In my family we tell great love stories.

Every family has a central narrative—the story they tell more often than any other. In my family it is the story of the great grandmother who stopped a railroad for the sake of love—not one or two trains, a whole railroad. We also have a story of a young girl who dreamed as she sat swinging on the front gate, hoping that a guy on the white horse who would take her away from a bad situation and, one day, he showed up, white horse and all. We also have a World War II romance, complete with love letters, a mid-19th Century shipboard romance, and a more distance tale of lost love and Viking pirates.  Given those stories, I can’t imagine the women in my family settling for less than love, which also means some of us spend our whole lives waiting . . .


Because the stories we tell often become the scripts we live or try to live?

That is a theme in Surprised by Story. More than that, Surprised by Story is about the power of family stories. It urges the reader to examine her own stories as means of taking control of her life. Stories can be reshaped. In fact, families do it all the time.
