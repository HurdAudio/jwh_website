Bidding Adieu to Sunbonnet Sue

 

Bidding Adieu to Sunbonnet Sue
OR
What's New About Writing the Women's West

From Roundup Magazine, December 1995

By Jerrie Hurd

There's a new woman riding the range. She isn't Owen Wister's schoolmarm, and she's not the gun-toting molls Hollywood recently gave us in the movie Bad Girls. Real women of the West have never been stand-ins for their men. Or shadows of them.

We always knew that.

What's different is the current excitement attending the rediscovery of women in the American West. I'm not talking about historical revisionism or political correctness. Nobody is whining that these stones have been neglected and ought to be told or taught I'm talking about the growing recognition that stones of real women in the American West have freshness and broad appeal. In fact "The Women's West" has become such a phenomenon some publishers, such as Pocket Books, are calling it a new genre and some bookstores, such as the Tattered Cover in Denver, are shelving it under its own category "The Women's West" is a term borrowed from the ground breaking book of the same title edited by Susan Armitage and Elizabeth Jameson that came out from the University of Oklahoma Press eight years ago. That book and a subsequent flood of scholarly books on the same subject, have presented such a strikingly different view of women in the American West that many people describe their first encounter with this new perspective as if they'd experienced an epiphany Most can name the book that first exposed them to it For me, it was reading Glendon Swarthout's Spur-award winning novel, The Homesman.

What's different about this view? It's more than discovering that women played broader rules than just being ranch wives or prostitutes. It enlivens on all levels taking in the diversity of women of all cultures and all time periods. More, it acknowledges the rich variety of ways with which women responded to the western experience. And beyond that, there's a shift in attitude that's integral and specific to this region of the United States that has so shaped our national identity.

When it first came out in 1985, I read the entire Norton Anthology of Literature by Women. I was looking for a sense of my own tradition, but I didn't find it, at least not in that volume. The Anthology contained a lot of nice writing. There was much I admired. Yet by the tune I reached the end, I was truly troubled by the fact that the predominate theme was victimization. In various ways, most of the stories explored over and over how women dealt will being victims and still managed to maintain some integral core of their being. That's fine, and an important story in its own right, but it doesn't resonate with my own experience growing-up in the American West.

I come from a long line of strong western women. In the 1880's, my great grandmother hired on with the railroad when she was 14 years old. She and a crew of women cooked for the men who were laying railroad trucks. The women fixed dinner and breakfast the next morning in one place. Then while the men were laying truck, the women packed up their tent kitchen, loaded it on mules, and moved it five, six, eight miles down the road bed, depending on how much track was going to be put down that day. Then the women unpacked, set up the tent again, and had dinner ready that evening by the time the men caught tip with them. Of course they had no microwaves. No modem conveniences. They didn't even have running water.

That's one of the places where we all used to get hung up on stories of women in the West. We used to emphasize the hardship of all that work with no running water. The West was "hell on women and horses," the old saying goes. Yet I remember my great-grandmother describing her experience as exciting. And she wasn't alone.

"I feel like urging them every one to get out and file on land. I am very enthusiastic about women homesteading," Elinore Pruitt Stewart wrote in her Letters from a Woman Homesteader. Then acknowledging that it could be difficult at times, she added, "but I realize temperament has much to do with success in any undertaking, and persons afraid of coyotes and work and loneliness had better let ranching alone."

My great-grandmother would have agreed. She said that women came in two types: those who "tied pretty bows" and those who "killed their own snakes." What's more, she took some pride in the fact that no one had ever accused her of " tying pretty bows." She quit cooking for railroad crews to homestead her own place and over a 45-year period expanded her ranch to sizable proportions. My mother now runs the Largest remaining piece of that ranch. For several generation now it has been a matter of some pride that none of the women in my family have ever been accused of tying pretty bows." We all solve our own problems, "kill our own snakes." On one level that sounds silly and simplistic, but in another sense everyone knows that the stones we tell each other make a real difference in our lives because stones create expectations. That was certainly true in my family and not just for the women. It takes a far more interesting man to interact with a strong woman than the man who's simply "taking care of the little lady."

In short, unlike the stones in the Norton Anthology I grew up in a tradition where women were expected to figure out how NOT to be victims, no matter the circumstances. I think that was largely true of frontier women, and because it's human nature to prefer winners over losers, I dunk my own family and thus my western heritage, are more attractive to a wider audience.

Hollywood seems to have discovered this new approach to the stories of women. Take, for instance, the recent role Meryl Streep played in The River Wild where her wilderness skills made the difference. Or for that matter the supporting role Sandra Bullock played in Speed where her coolness and concentration counted. In a recent Rolling Stone Interview Demi Moore elaborated on why she felt taking her part in Disclosure was a smart career move, saying its a recognition of where the interest in women's stories is going.

Print has echoed this change as well. The Economist in the September 24, 1994, issue, makes the same point in an article entitled "The New Eve Is No Angel." That article notes that between Glenn Close's Fatal Attraction and Sharon Stone's Basic Instinct there is a noticeable shift. Stone is not a pregnant woman scorned and deranged. Guilty or not, she takes charge of whatever situation she finds herself in. "What are you going to do?" she asks when she's told not to fight up at the police station where she's being questioned for murder "Charge me with smoking?" And then she lights up.

There's a growing sense that fiction today needs some of that same attitude. In March Publishers Weekly analyzed the softness in the sales of traditional women's fiction and quoted Barbara Morrow, co-owner of Northshire Bookstore in Manchester, Vermont, as saying, "The response in the store to a lot of tides was 'Who cares? There's so much repetition in these books. They are boring the audience' Margaret Maupin of Denver's Tattered Cover Bookstore agreed, "It's been very hard this year to go through fiction and help people find books. It's pretty flat" The article goes on to discuss the fact that while publishers are still making money on women's fiction, dig are experiencing higher than usual rates of returns even on well known authors' books. The old stories just aren't selling like they used to.
No wonder. The Post-Boom generation, born between 1961 and 1981, is beginning to have an impact across the board and that includes book buying. These so-called Generation Xer's were described in US News and Word Report in February as being "pragmatic, non-ideological, entrepreneurial, and action oriented" They see themselves as "a survival generation of latchkey children" who learned early to fend for themselves and "do end-mm around conventions that only get in the way." That description could as aptly fit the emerging view recent scholarship has created of women in the American West. Perhaps the current Interest in the "Women's West" can best be explained by that resonance. Like women today, real women of the American West shatter stereotypes. Finding themselves thrust outside the Victorian culture of their mothers, they had to make their way in a "man's worldwide pluck and stamina and for the most part they enjoyed that new found "freedom."

The broad appeal of these stories is demonstrated by the fact that Cindy Bonner's recent "women's west' novels Lily and Looking After Lily, have been successfully marketed as both adult and young adult fiction. Likewise Sandra Cisneros, Linda Hogan, and Leslie Marmon Silko have found wide readership for their novels with ethnic emphasis, and Barbara Kingsolver's contemporary western novels have found a large appreciative audience.

All of which brings us back to that new woman riding the range who's neither schoolmarm nor bad girl. How is she best described?

A survey of current fiction would indicate that these women protagonists can shoot and ride and play poker with the best, but they don't rely on those skiffs alone and that allows them to remain feminine. It also makes them more believable than the old stock figures. On the other hand, they are not victims and, for that reason, are less internal with their concerns than traditional heroines of women's fiction. Nevertheless, they're likely to have a keen sense of themselves. Having borrowed from both the western and romance traditions these new women protagonists have struck a fresh balance. They worry less and do more, and what they do, they do with an unapologetic gutsy determination. In short, they're the kind of women we always knew peopled the West, the kind of women we've always loved.

Jerrie Hurd's latest novel, Kate Burke Shoots the Old West (Pocket Books) embodies the best of the "Women's West" as did her earlier release Miss Ellie's Purple Sage Saloon (Pocket Books). She is President of Women Writing the West, is on the Board of Directors for the Women of the West Museum, and lives in Boulder, Colorado.
