Gather Family History Outline

Just Above Bone is a memoir that explores the power of family stories.
Want to explore your own family stories?

First things first. Before you can gather family stories, you need to assess what you already know.

MAKE A PLAN
Write a brief family history.
While you're writing this history, note what you know and don't know and make notes of gaps you'd like to fill in.

  • Make a list of who can help you fill in the stories.
  • What photographs, letters, or other family artifacts do you have?
    Who has items you might want to copy of photograph?
  • What traditions does your family observe?
    Try to assess why some traditions have survived while others haven't? Are stories hidden in those traditions? What would you like your children or other members of your family to continue to observe? Can you articulate why?
  • Question your own motives.
    Write a letter to yourself about why you want to gather your family's stories. Put this letter away. After you've finished all the steps in this guide, read the letter and note how much you and your ideas about your family’s history have changed. That will be a story in itself.
  • Make a plan.
    Decide on the first three things you'd like to do in pursuit of your family's stories.


TRY AN INTERVIEW
Every interview is different and should be. Knowing how to get the most out of an interviews is as much art as skill. It helps to remember that an interview should be fun, like a good conversation. Knowing how to write-up an interview is also part of the art.

  • Interview a friend you'd like to know better.
    This will give you a chance to discover how you are most comfortable talking to someone. Will you take notes? Use a tape recorder? Take someone along with you?
  • Write up your test interview.
    Notice what material you wish you had. How could you have gotten a fuller interview? Try going back and filling in, but also remember, each interview will be different and how you write it up will also be different.
  • Make an appointment to interview someone in your family and then do it.
  • Here's something interesting—just to try. Do an imaginary interview with someone whose dead. What's the value in this imaginary interview?

GO HOME
Most stories are connected to a place. One reason to collect family stories is to keep yourself and your family connected to a place and a past-grounded in some real sense.

  • Write an essay about your home.
    Where's home for you? In our mobile world, "home" is increasingly difficult to identify. Are you having trouble with this one? Maybe you have more than one place that you call home. If your family immigrated recently, there may be a place far away and one that is closer now. Whatever your situation, write about it. Try writing about the particular place you live now and then in the larger sense of how you identify yourself as being from somewhere.
  • Write an origin story of your family.
    When and where does your family begin. How did you get to the place you call "home."
  • Find and re-tell one folk story from "home."


CONNECT YOURSELF TO HISTORY

  • Pick an event in history that affected your family. Research the event until you feel fairly certain you know the official facts.
  • Can you fit your family's story into the larger narrative?
    Sometimes, this can be difficult. Official versions of history may not always be right or may not always include the same detail as family versions. Can you figure out how to check the facts of your family story and of the official version and vice versa? Official history often has been shaped by historians who had their own reasons for telling the story as they did. Same with family versions. At some point you'll have to decide what standard of truth you will apply to your family stories. In your fact checking, you'll need to be more careful, but don't always assume that the family version is wrong. Dig. Do the detective work. There may be times when you cannot establish the truth. Then what will you do?
  • In what ways has the media or popular culture taken over your family's history and turned it into something that it isn't? For example, people who grew up on ranches don’t necessarily connect with the movie version of cowboys. Can you write a few paragraphs about this problem?


WHAT ABOUT YOU?
Family history is happening all the time. If you don't capture your own part in that unfolding drama, you will leave a gap as big as the ones you've been trying to fill with your research.

  • Write a factual account of your life.
    This will cover the important dates and places and parts of current events that you witnessed or participated in. Don't be afraid to express your opinions on the things you saw and did. You will also need to check your facts. No one remembers things perfectly.
  • Capture yourself as a myth.
    Who are you really? Under the facts, how do you see yourself? Very likely you are different in different situations. Maybe you need to write more than one myth of yourself. Think about what you'd really like someone else to know about you and your life.
  • Read the letter you in the first exercise. Add something about what's changed about you since then.

    Keep in mind that this is just a beginning. You will have other ideas of what you need to do to collect your family's stories.
